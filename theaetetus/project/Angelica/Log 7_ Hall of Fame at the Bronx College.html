Log 7: Hall of Fame at the Bronx College

March 10, 2018

After a week of snow, it was rewarding to finally accomplish going to the place that I had to postpone going to due to the storm. The Hall of Fame for Great Americans at the Bronx Community College was the perfect end to a week-long Greek adventure.
With the wind howling, the buildings quiet, and the sky set with just a touch of gray, I embarked towards the Hall of Fame for Great Americans. I didnt know what exactly to expect. From google, the space looked like a bunch of busts crowded together in a sort of monotonous harmony. In reality, it is. But its also so much more.

If this Hall of Fame communicates anything about America, then the message is this: America is full of visionaries, and people who are proud of them. In all, this Hall is full of authors, scientists, inventors, and leaders. These are the sort of people who are praised not only in this hall, but also in history and in America. They are people who contribute their talents to the society they live in, and sometimes, their contributions dare change that society for the better. If they did a good job, then their name is probably here, nestled above the Harlem River in a college made for the community. 
Like ancient Greece, this place and the sculptures within its pillars were built to last forever. Its a symbol of legacy, and a gesture of gratefulness towards those who made their life one to remember.

I thought it was peculiar, however, that there were noticeably two busts missing. I had my assumptions of course. One of them, was that the sculptures removed may have been ones representing Americans who promoted slavery. After a quick google search, I found out that my initial assumption was right. Robert E. Lee, and Stonewall Jackson were both removed as they were confederate generals, therefore not represented or worthy of a position as a great American.

This made me think about the figures represented in famous Greek sculptures. Though many were of those who were seen as great, such as gods and rulers, Greek art also included replicated accounts, sometimes of battles, and sometimes of other negative aspects of life as well. Even though some of the sculpture scenes in Greek art are negative, they were created as reminders of the unfortunate past, almost as a warning to the future. This way of thinking and creating was very different from the kind that created the Hall of Fame for Great Americans. This hall had a very specific goal in mind, and when new realizations about certain individuals made them unfit, the necessary removal was made. In this place, the art does not last forever if the meaning it holds does not represent the place it stands for and within. In the end, the values determine the art presented, and the art was not simply made to represent history.


 After spending a week exploring the different ways Greece is intertwined in New York, I have realized that Greek architecture was the natural choice for most architects in New York as well. Greek elements, such as the open-air colonnade, were used to not only mimic the grandeur of Greek temples, but to also house the sculptures within them. Building temples or buildings with the purpose of housing sculptures is an extremely ancient Greek notion, and it is clearly one that is both practically and cleverly used in this Hall of Fame.

Greek elements were used in the construction of this landmark because Greek architecture was largely based on serving and praising Gods. In a similar way, this landmark was created in order to house people who are also idols- looked up to, and praised as heroes. This is a sort of temple. One, not where priests or kings enter to give sacrifices, but a temple of the great humans of the past. A temple where great people can be admired for doing great things, so that just maybe, someone else can walk through and dream of a legacy which will also always remain sculpted.

end of log.
-Angelica
